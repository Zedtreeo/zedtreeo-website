The Challenge

The consultancy specializes in foundation design, slope stability analysis, and environmental site assessments across commercial, infrastructure, and energy projects. With 72 engineers across four offices and $28M in annual revenue, the firm was winning more work than it could deliver. A 34-project backlog was growing by 5–8 new engagements per month, report turnaround had stretched to six weeks, and two municipal clients had issued formal complaints about missed deliverable deadlines.

1

Report turnaround was delaying project milestones

Average geotechnical report delivery had stretched from 3 weeks to 6 weeks over the previous 18 months. The bottleneck sat at the data-processing and drafting stage — borehole logs needed manual entry into gINT, lab results required cross-referencing against ASTM standards, and every report needed 8–12 AutoCAD figures. Two DOT contracts carried liquidated damages clauses tied to report delivery dates, putting $340K in penalty exposure on the table.

2

Senior engineers were trapped in data entry

Time-tracking data showed PE-licensed engineers spending 40% of their billable hours on borehole log entry, lab data formatting, and GIS layer preparation — tasks that required technical knowledge but not professional engineering judgment. At the firm’s $185 blended billing rate, each senior engineer was consuming $148K annually in non-value-added data processing work instead of analysis, design, and client advisory.

3

Project backlog was eroding client retention

The firm carried 34 active site investigations with only 12 staff members available for data processing and drafting. Three repeat clients representing $4.2M in annual billings had requested timeline guarantees the firm could not make. The business development team reported losing two competitive bids specifically because prospective clients cited the firm’s recent delivery track record as a disqualifier.

4

Hiring locally was too slow and too expensive

A US-based geotechnical data analyst cost $78K–$95K fully loaded. A CAD/GIS drafter with civil engineering experience ran $72K–$88K. The firm needed five hires to clear the backlog and sustain 3x throughput, but the local talent pool for gINT- and PLAXIS-proficient analysts was thin — the last two postings sat open for 14 weeks with only six qualified applicants between them.
