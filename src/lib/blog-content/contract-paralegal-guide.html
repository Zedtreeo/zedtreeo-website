What Does a Contract Paralegal Do?

A contract paralegal performs the same substantive legal work as an in-house paralegal—the difference is the employment relationship. They work on a temporary, project-based, or ongoing contract basis rather than as a permanent employee. Here are the core functions:

Litigation Support

Managing discovery requests, organizing exhibits, preparing trial binders, tracking deadlines through case management software, and coordinating with opposing counsel on scheduling. For firms handling multi-party litigation, a contract paralegal can manage the entire discovery workflow independently.

Document Review and Management

Reviewing large document sets for relevance, privilege, and responsiveness. This includes e-discovery platforms like Relativity, Concordance, and Logikcull. A skilled contract paralegal can review 50–75 documents per hour with consistent accuracy—making them essential for any case involving significant document production.

Legal Research

Conducting case law research, statutory analysis, and regulatory review using Westlaw, LexisNexis, and public legal databases. Contract paralegals draft research memoranda, compile case summaries, and identify precedent relevant to your matter.

Case Preparation and Filing

Drafting pleadings, motions, discovery responses, and correspondence under attorney supervision. Preparing and filing documents with state and federal courts, including e-filing through CM/ECF and state-specific electronic systems. Managing filing deadlines, service requirements, and court rules.

Client Communication and Intake

Conducting initial client interviews, gathering case facts, managing client communication logs, and coordinating with witnesses. For firms that handle high-volume practice areas (personal injury, immigration, family law), contract paralegals can manage the entire intake pipeline.

Remote vs On-Site Contract Paralegals: Why Remote Is Winning

The debate between remote and on-site contract paralegals effectively ended in 2024. Here's why remote has become the dominant model for contract paralegal work:

The Work Is Already Digital

Paralegal work in 2026 is overwhelmingly screen-based. Document review happens in cloud platforms. Legal research runs through online databases. Court filings are electronic. Case management is SaaS-based. The percentage of paralegal tasks that genuinely require physical presence in an office has dropped below 10%—and those tasks (witness interviews, court appearances, physical document handling) can be assigned to in-house staff while a remote paralegal handles everything else.

Cost Advantage Is Decisive

An on-site contract paralegal in New York, Chicago, or Los Angeles commands $50–$75/hour. A remote contract paralegal with equivalent skills and experience costs $8–$15/hour. The 70–90% cost savings from outsourcing aren't marginal—they're transformative for a firm's profitability per case.

Talent Pool Is Global

Hiring on-site limits you to your metro area. Hiring remote gives you access to paralegals who have worked with US law firms for years, understand American legal systems, and bring specialized expertise that may not exist locally. Many remote paralegals hold US-recognized certifications and have trained on the same tools your firm uses.

When On-Site Still Makes Sense

There are legitimate scenarios where on-site presence matters: court hearings requiring physical attendance, in-person client meetings in sensitive matters, handling original documents or evidence, and situations where attorney-client privilege concerns require controlled physical access. For everything else, remote is more efficient and dramatically cheaper.

Skills to Evaluate When Hiring a Contract Paralegal

Not all paralegals are interchangeable. The skills that matter depend on your practice area, but here's the evaluation framework that consistently identifies high-performers:

Technical Proficiency

  • Legal research platforms: Westlaw, LexisNexis, Bloomberg Law, PACER
  • E-discovery tools: Relativity, Concordance, Logikcull, Disco
  • Case management: Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther, Filevine
  • Document management: NetDocuments, iManage, SharePoint
  • Court filing systems: CM/ECF, state-specific e-filing platforms

Substantive Legal Knowledge

  • Jurisdiction awareness: Understanding of state vs federal rules, local court procedures, and filing requirements specific to the jurisdictions you practice in
  • Practice area depth: Litigation paralegals need different skills than corporate, immigration, or estate planning paralegals. A generalist can handle basic tasks, but complex work demands specialization
  • Legal writing quality: Request writing samples. A strong paralegal produces clear, well-organized memoranda that reduce attorney review time rather than creating more work

Professional Certifications

  • CP (Certified Paralegal): Issued by NALA, requires passing a comprehensive exam
  • RP (Registered Paralegal): Issued by NFPA, demonstrates advanced competency
  • PP (Professional Paralegal): NALA's advanced certification for experienced paralegals
  • State-specific certifications: Some states (California, Florida, Texas) have additional certification programs

Soft Skills That Matter

  • Attention to detail: One missed deadline or filing error can be catastrophic. Test this during vetting with a practical exercise, not just interview questions
  • Communication clarity: Contract paralegals work with less direct supervision. They need to flag issues proactively, ask precise questions, and provide clear status updates
  • Confidentiality discipline: Legal work involves sensitive information. Verify their understanding of confidentiality obligations and data handling protocols

The Vetting Process: A Step-by-Step Framework

Whether you're hiring through a staffing agency, a freelance platform, or a dedicated remote provider, use this vetting sequence to avoid costly hiring mistakes:

Step 1: Define the Scope Before You Search

Before posting a single job listing or contacting a provider, document exactly what you need: practice area, specific tasks, required tools/platforms, expected hours, duration of engagement, and jurisdiction requirements. Vague requirements produce vague candidates.

Step 2: Screen for Relevant Experience

Filter candidates based on practice area match, not just "paralegal experience." A litigation paralegal with 5 years in personal injury may be completely unprepared for corporate M&A work. Ask for case examples that mirror your actual needs.

Step 3: Practical Skills Assessment

Give candidates a paid test assignment that reflects real work they'll do. For document review: provide a small document set and evaluate their coding accuracy and speed. For legal research: assign a research question and assess the quality of their memo. For drafting: request a sample motion or discovery response.

Step 4: Reference Checks with Attorneys

Contact supervising attorneys from previous engagements, not just HR contacts. Ask about accuracy, deadline reliability, independence level, and how they handled corrections or feedback. One reference from an attorney who worked directly with the paralegal is worth more than three generic HR references.

Step 5: Trial Period

Start with a defined trial period (1–2 weeks for project work, 2–4 weeks for ongoing engagements). Set clear deliverables and evaluation criteria before the trial begins. This is where best practices for hiring remote staff become critical—structured onboarding and clear expectations make or break the first month.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

After placing hundreds of legal professionals, these are the warning signs that consistently predict poor outcomes:

  • Can't name specific tools: If a paralegal claims litigation experience but can't name the e-discovery platform they used, they're inflating their resume
  • Vague on jurisdiction: "I've worked on federal cases" without being able to specify which districts, rules, or filing systems is a red flag
  • No writing samples available: Every working paralegal has samples they can share (redacted). Refusal or inability to provide them suggests limited substantive experience
  • Resistance to test assignments: Qualified professionals welcome the opportunity to demonstrate their skills. Resistance usually signals insecurity about ability
  • Overpromising turnaround times: A paralegal who promises to review 200 documents per hour or complete complex research in 2 hours is either cutting corners or doesn't understand the work
  • No questions about your systems: A good contract paralegal asks about your case management software, filing conventions, communication preferences, and supervision structure. No questions means they're not thinking about integration

Data Security and Confidentiality for Remote Legal Staff

Legal work involves privileged information, client confidential data, and documents subject to court protective orders. Any contract paralegal arrangement—remote or otherwise—must address these security requirements:

  • NDAs and confidentiality agreements: Executed before any access to client files or case information
  • Encrypted communications: All file transfers and messaging through encrypted channels (not personal email)
  • Access controls: Role-based permissions in your document management and case management systems. The contract paralegal sees only the matters they're assigned to
  • Device security: Remote paralegals should work on secured devices with updated antivirus, encrypted storage, and no unauthorized software. Managed providers like Zedtreeo enforce this at the infrastructure level
  • Data residency awareness: If you handle matters with data localization requirements (GDPR, certain government contracts), verify that your remote paralegal's work setup complies
  • Audit trail: Use platforms that log document access, edits, and downloads. If a protective order requires you to account for who accessed what, you need this documentation

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What is a contract paralegal?

A contract paralegal is a legal professional who works on a temporary, project-based, or ongoing contract basis rather than as a permanent employee. They perform the same substantive work as in-house paralegals—document review, legal research, case preparation, filing—but without the long-term employment commitment, benefits costs, or office overhead.

Q2: How much does a contract paralegal cost?

US-based contract paralegals cost $35–$75/hour through staffing agencies or $25–$60/hour as freelancers. Q3: What is the difference between a contract paralegal and a freelance paralegal?

The terms are often used interchangeably, but there's a practical distinction. A freelance paralegal operates independently, finds their own clients, and manages their own business. A contract paralegal typically works through a staffing agency or managed provider, which handles placement, vetting, and infrastructure. Managed contract arrangements generally offer more accountability and backup.

Q4: Can a remote contract paralegal handle state-specific filings?

Yes, provided they have experience with the relevant jurisdiction's rules and e-filing systems. Many remote paralegals have worked with US law firms for years and are trained on CM/ECF, state-specific platforms, and local court procedures. During vetting, verify jurisdiction-specific experience through practical assessments, not just resume claims.

Q5: How do I ensure confidentiality with a contract paralegal?

Execute NDAs before granting any system access. Use encrypted communication channels and role-based access controls. Require secured devices with encrypted storage. Work with managed providers that enforce security protocols at the infrastructure level. Maintain audit trails for all document access, especially for matters under protective orders.

Q6: When should I hire a full-time paralegal instead of a contract paralegal?

Hire full-time when you have consistent, year-round paralegal work that fills 30+ hours per week indefinitely. If your workload fluctuates seasonally, varies by case volume, or involves specialized projects with defined timelines, contract paralegals offer better cost efficiency and flexibility. Many firms use a hybrid model: one full-time paralegal plus contract support during surges.

Q7: What certifications should a contract paralegal have?

The most recognized certifications are NALA's Certified Paralegal (CP), NFPA's Registered Paralegal (RP), and state-specific credentials. However, certifications alone don't guarantee competence. A practical skills assessment that mirrors your actual work is a more reliable quality indicator than any credential. Use certifications as a screening filter, not a hiring decision.
