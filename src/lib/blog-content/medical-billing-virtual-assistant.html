What Does a Medical Billing Virtual Assistant Do?

A medical billing VA handles the end-to-end revenue cycle for your practice, from the moment a patient is seen to the moment payment is collected. Here's the full scope:

Charge Entry and Claim Preparation

Entering charges from superbills or EHR encounter data into the practice management system. Verifying CPT, ICD-10, and HCPCS codes match the documented services. Ensuring modifier usage is accurate and payer-specific billing rules are followed. For a practice with 50–100 encounters per day, charge entry alone occupies 2–3 hours of focused work daily.

Claim Submission

Submitting clean claims to payers through clearinghouses (Availity, Waystar, Trizetto, Change Healthcare). Monitoring submission confirmations and addressing rejected claims within 24 hours. Tracking submission-to-payment timelines by payer to identify patterns.

Insurance Verification and Eligibility

Verifying patient insurance eligibility and benefits before or at the time of service. Confirming coverage details, copay amounts, deductible status, and prior authorization requirements. This function alone prevents a significant portion of claim denials—MGMA data shows that eligibility-related denials account for 25–30% of all claim denials.

Denial Management

This is where a skilled billing VA earns their cost back many times over. Denial management involves: identifying denied claims by denial reason code, determining whether the denial is correctable (coding error, missing information) or requires a formal appeal, preparing and submitting appeals with supporting documentation, and tracking appeal outcomes. A dedicated denial specialist can recover 60–75% of denied revenue that would otherwise be written off.

Payment Posting

Processing electronic remittance advice (ERA) and explanation of benefits (EOB) documents. Posting payments to patient accounts. Identifying underpayments by comparing paid amounts against contracted rates. Flagging discrepancies for follow-up. Accurate payment posting is the foundation of clean AR—errors here cascade into every downstream report.

Accounts Receivable Follow-Up

Working the AR aging report systematically: prioritizing high-dollar claims, following up on unpaid claims at defined intervals (30, 60, 90 days), contacting payers for status updates, and escalating to supervisors when patterns indicate systemic issues. The benchmark is keeping AR >120 days below 10% of total receivables.

Patient Billing and Collections

Generating and sending patient statements. Responding to patient billing inquiries. Setting up payment plans. Following up on patient balances. Managing the patient portion of revenue—which has grown significantly as high-deductible health plans have shifted more cost to patients.

Required Skills and Certifications

Not every "medical billing VA" on a freelance platform can actually do the job. Here's what to screen for:

Essential Technical Skills

  • CPT, ICD-10, HCPCS coding: Understanding of code selection, modifier usage, and documentation requirements. The biller doesn't need to code from scratch (that's the coder's job), but they must recognize coding errors that cause denials
  • EHR/Practice management proficiency: Experience with the specific system your practice uses. The major platforms each have different billing workflows:
    • Epic: Resolv for claim management, MyChart for patient billing
    • Cerner: Revenue Cycle Management module
    • AdvancedMD: Integrated billing and scheduling
    • Kareo: Cloud-based billing optimized for small practices
    • Athenahealth: athenaCollector for claims and AR
    • eClinicalWorks: Integrated RCM module
  • Clearinghouse navigation: Proficiency with Availity, Waystar, or Trizetto for claim submission, status checking, and ERA processing
  • Payer portal knowledge: Ability to navigate Medicare, Medicaid, and commercial payer portals for claim status, prior authorization, and eligibility verification

Certifications to Look For

Certification Issuing Body What It Demonstrates Relevance
CPC (Certified Professional Coder) AAPC Coding knowledge and billing fundamentals High—most widely recognized
CPB (Certified Professional Biller) AAPC Billing-specific expertise including AR and denials High—directly relevant to billing VA role
CCS (Certified Coding Specialist) AHIMA Advanced coding for hospital and facility billing Moderate—more relevant for facility billing
CMRS (Certified Medical Reimbursement Specialist) AMBA Reimbursement methodology and compliance Moderate—good for denial management focus

Practical note: Certifications indicate foundational knowledge but don't guarantee practical competence. A biller with 3 years of hands-on experience in your specialty but no certification will typically outperform a freshly certified biller with no real-world experience. Use certifications as a screening filter, then evaluate practical skills through a test assignment.

Specialty-Specific Knowledge

Medical billing complexity varies dramatically by specialty. A biller experienced in family medicine may struggle with orthopedic surgery billing. Key specialty considerations:

  • Primary care/Family medicine: High-volume E&M coding, preventive care billing, chronic care management (CCM) codes
  • Surgery: Complex modifier usage, global period rules, co-surgery and assistant surgeon billing
  • Mental health: Time-based coding, telehealth parity rules, prior authorization-heavy payers
  • Cardiology: Procedure-heavy coding, device-dependent billing, diagnostic testing codes
  • Dermatology: Biopsy billing, destruction codes, Mohs surgery coding
  • OB/GYN: Global obstetric packages, antepartum visit bundling, prenatal care coding

HIPAA Compliance Requirements

Any medical billing VA—remote or otherwise—handles protected health information (PHI) every day. HIPAA compliance isn't optional. Here's the minimum framework:

Before the VA Starts Work

  • BAA execution: If hiring through a provider (Zedtreeo, staffing agency), the provider executes a Business Associate Agreement covering all their staff. If hiring a freelancer directly, the individual must sign a BAA
  • HIPAA training: Completed and documented before any system access is granted
  • NDA execution: Covering all patient information and practice data
  • Background check: Verify no history of healthcare fraud, HIPAA violations, or relevant criminal activity

During the Engagement

  • Encrypted access only: VPN or zero-trust connection to your systems. No direct internet access to EHR or billing platforms
  • Secured work environment: Dedicated workspace, no shared screens, no printing of PHI
  • Minimum necessary access: The billing VA should have access only to billing-relevant patient data, not complete medical records
  • Audit logging: All PHI access logged and reviewed periodically
  • Annual HIPAA refresher training: Document completion annually

When to Hire a Billing VA vs Outsource to a Billing Company

This is the most common decision point for practices evaluating their billing options. Here's a clear framework:

Hire a Medical Billing VA When:

  • You want direct control over your revenue cycle and billing processes
  • You have an office manager or revenue cycle lead who can supervise billing operations
  • Your claim volume is consistent enough to justify a dedicated biller (typically 100+ claims/month)
  • You want to maintain visibility into every claim, denial, and payment
  • Cost optimization is a priority—a dedicated VA costs 70–90% less than a billing company based on percentage of collections

Outsource to a Billing Company When:

  • You have zero internal billing expertise and no one to supervise billing operations
  • Your practice is very small (<50 claims/month) and doesn't justify even a part-time dedicated biller
  • You're willing to pay a premium (4–10% of collections) for completely hands-off management
  • You're in a startup phase and need billing operational from day one without building internal processes

The Hybrid Approach

Many practices use a combination: a dedicated remote billing VA handles daily charge entry, claim submission, and payment posting, while a billing company or consultant handles complex appeals, payer contract negotiations, and compliance audits. This gives you cost control on routine operations and expert support for high-stakes decisions.

How to Hire a Medical Billing Virtual Assistant

The hiring process for a medical billing VA requires more diligence than a general virtual assistant because the stakes are higher—billing errors directly impact your revenue. Here's the step-by-step process:

  • Present a denied claim scenario and ask them to explain the appeal strategy
  • Ask them to walk through their process for working an AR aging report
  • Test their knowledge of your specific EHR system (or similar systems)

Start with a 1–2 week trial on a defined subset of your billing. Measure clean claim rate, turnaround time, and accuracy against your existing benchmarks. Zedtreeo offers a 5-day free trial specifically for this evaluation.

Provide documented SOPs for your billing workflows, payer-specific rules, and EHR navigation. Plan for 2–4 weeks of supervised operation with daily check-ins before transitioning to independent work. Review 100% of work in week 1, then reduce to sampling.

Common Hiring Mistakes

  • Hiring a generalist for specialty billing: A VA who bills for primary care may not understand surgical modifier rules, mental health time-based coding, or OB global packages. Match specialty experience to your practice
  • Skipping the practical assessment: Certifications and experience claims on a resume don't guarantee competence with your specific systems and workflows. Always test with real scenarios
  • No HIPAA compliance framework: Hiring a freelance biller without a BAA, NDA, or secured access protocol exposes your practice to regulatory risk
  • Expecting immediate full productivity: Even experienced billers need 2–4 weeks to learn your specific workflows, payer mix, and documentation conventions. Budget for this ramp-up period
  • Choosing on price alone: A biller at $5/hour who produces a 90% clean claim rate costs you more in denied revenue than a biller at $10/hour with a 97% clean claim rate. Always evaluate on outcomes, not hourly cost
